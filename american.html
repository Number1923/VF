The American Setting is the second major storyline of the VortexFoods™ universe, running parallel to the German Setting. Where the German Setting finds VortexFoods™ embedded in the everyday rhythms of student life and European society, the American Setting foregrounds the system's darker institutional architecture — the security apparatus, the activist resistance, the paramilitary enforcement apparatus, and the families who exist comfortably within it all.

The setting covers a wider physical range than the German Setting: a warm coastal suburb, a transatlantic flight route, a major processing facility, a beachfront Vortex restaurant, a snowbound Midwestern family home, and a Grand Imperial hotel. This variety reflects the American Setting's broader scope — it is less interested in one community and more in mapping how VortexFoods™ permeates American institutional life from all directions simultaneously.

The American Setting also intersects with the German Setting through Max — a German student who opposes Vortex on moral grounds, meets Ashley Taylor at passport control, and is swallowed by her unknowingly hours later — and through Ulrike Grimm and Alfred Hoffmann, German characters who appear aboard the transatlantic flight.

A Matter of Principle
AMERICAN SETTING · STORY 3

The most politically charged story in the American Setting. Paige Wright and her brother Dylan, along with around a dozen comrades, stage a night raid on one of Vortex's largest North American processing centres. Their mission is sabotaged from within by Seo-Yun Park — embedded as a Vortex informant — and the group is captured by Kayla Hayes and her paramilitary detail. They are sent straight to processing. The story's second half follows Hannah Lindquist, an incoming college freshman eating her first ever shrinkie at a beachfront Vortex restaurant — an internal moral struggle that turns out to be repressed arousal. Paige ends up on Hannah's tray; Rachel Cohen swallows Miguel; Ashley casually consumes Dylan after recognising who he is. The story ends with Hannah sleeping contentedly as her body digests her first dozen victims, her hand drifting to her stomach.

Checking Out
AMERICAN SETTING · STORY 5

A second-person story set in a Midwestern hotel. The protagonist — a business traveller told as "you" — is woken by a 6 AM wake-up call from Victoria Lindquist at the Grand Imperial front desk, checks out after a brief charged flirtation, and is subsequently shrunk and packaged into a Vortex box Victoria takes home. Multiple perspectives intercut: the protagonist watching from inside the box as Victoria's bath consumes occupant after occupant; Hannah on an early morning video call casually eating cereal laced with shrinkies; Cassandra Davis eating from the staff's complimentary Vortex delivery with her morning coffee. The protagonist's escape attempt into the bath water triggers Victoria's most theatrical moment — she submerges, waits, then erupts from the water above them, mouth open wide, performing a shark-dive she learned from her sister-in-law Stacy. They are swallowed whole and die slowly overnight as she sleeps. Kyle Edwards survives the bath only to be given to Stacy two days later.

Setting Themes & Analysis

Institutional Power & Section V

The American Setting's most distinctive lore contribution is Section V — a protocol revealed in An American Nightmare that allows border control and law enforcement officers to hand detained individuals directly to Vortex operatives with no judicial oversight. Max's arrest and transformation from a person Ashley just met into a product she unknowingly consumes within hours is the series' starkest illustration of how the system swallows individuals without friction. His social media profile vanishes from the internet as she finishes eating.

The Resistance

The American Setting contains the only direct depiction of organised resistance to VortexFoods™. Paige Wright's activist group in A Matter of Principle represents an ideologically serious attempt to fight back — and is dismantled not through superior force but through infiltration. Seo-Yun Park's betrayal illustrates the futility that defines the resistance's position: even the people inside the movement may be working for Vortex. Kayla Hayes's contemptuous dismissal of Seo-Yun's legal deal for the captured activists — "Watch me" — encapsulates the system's relationship to the rule of law.

The Lindquist Arc

The Lindquist family functions as the American Setting's most sustained character study. Hannah is introduced as an apparently moral young woman whose horror at eating shrinkies in A Matter of Principle turns out to be repressed arousal — and across the next two stories she evolves into the setting's most intimately predatory figure. Her sister Victoria's arc in Checking Out provides a counterpoint: a more complex consumer who feels genuine if suppressible guilt, who was raised on clean-kill ethics, and who performs the shark-dive not out of sadism but something more theatrical and self-aware. Their father is a military officer; their brother a police officer. The Lindquist family is the American Setting's portrait of how institutional authority and normalised predation coexist within a single family unit.

Tragic Irony as Structure

The American Setting returns repeatedly to the same structural irony: the predator and victim share a genuine human connection, and that connection changes nothing. Ashley and Max bond at passport control; she eats him that evening. The Checking Out protagonist and Victoria share real mutual attraction at the front desk; she surfaces from the bathwater over them hours later. Arthur carried a years-long crush on Hannah; she uses him for pleasure and swallows him whole. The universe is not cruel in the way of a slasher — it is indifferent, which is worse.
